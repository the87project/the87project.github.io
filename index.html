The 87 What Britain actually thinks, one double-decker at a time.

Put 87 people on one double-decker and ask them the big questions.

A standard London double-decker bus seats exactly 87 people: 40 on the upper deck and 47 on the lower deck. By mapping abstract public opinion data to these physical seats, we turn cold percentages into a tangible, human-scale reality.

About this project

Why 87 people?

Human beings aren't wired to understand large-scale statistics. In psychology, “innumeracy” and “psychic numbing” describe our evolutionary inability to emotionally register what numbers like “4%” or “47%” actually represent when applied to millions of citizens.

When a poll says 4% of the country holds a view, it feels negligible. But when you map that to a standard British double-decker bus, that 4% represents over three seats. Imagine getting onto your morning commute and seeing three real people sitting right at the front, passionately holding that exact view — suddenly, the statistic becomes a social reality you can picture.

Furthermore, 87 is a mathematically stubborn number. Unlike 100, it doesn't cleanly divide into neat, round intervals. This is a deliberate feature: because your brain cannot effortlessly convert 87 back into a clinical percentage, it forces you to look at the visual data for what it truly represents — an indivisible group of human beings sharing a single space.

About the numbers. Figures are drawn from publicly reported polling. Each question is redrawn as one full double-decker bus of 87 people — the capacity of a New Routemaster (40 upper deck, 47 below) — so one passenger represents ~1.15% of respondents; rounding applies, and no group that registered in the source polling is ever rounded off the bus entirely. All commentary is original and independent, and every entry links to its source. Built as a personal data & policy project.
